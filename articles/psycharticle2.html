Ageing without dementia

can stimulating psychosocial and lifestyle experiences make a difference?

March 19, 2021 Rebecca Tailleu ARTICLE


This article gives a summary of the latest articles published around healthier and longer living, that is without dementia. Information was gathered from PubMed, an online library of medicine containing literature from science journals, books or other online biomedical sources.

The article explains two interesting mechanisms. First, the article explains the interaction between environmental and genetic influences. Second, it sums up the preventive strategies in dementia. In addition, evidence of risk factors influencing aging and dementia from three life periods are given.

Interaction of environmental and genetic influences 

Aging is an accumulation of biological deficits results from genetically and environmentally induced alterations. This leads to decrease in homoeostatic balance, which leads to progressive physical and cognitive impairment. Thus, aging can be considered as an equilibrium between multifactorial stressors (environment) and biological resilience or repair mechanisms (genes). Although dementia is an age-dependent condition, it’s neither a normal part of again. Some people who are 100 years old are free from dementia. So different risk factors play a role in dementia, but symptoms emerge only after a decades-long process. 

Preventive strategies in dementia from three life periods (risk factors) 

  • Early life [0-25 years]

Childhood factors:  Childhood negative life events increase the likelihood of dementia (e.g. low childhood SES or socioeconomic status, prenatal environments, unhealthy diet). These events lead to increase in depression, stress-induced dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis or unhealthy behaviors. These are risk factors of dementia. 

Education:   Low educational level has been linked with increased risk of dementia. There is a 7% decline in dementia risk per each additional year of schooling.

  • Midlife [26-59 years]

Work complexity and job strain:  Occupational environments play an important role. Individuals with jobs characterized with high complexity had decreased hippocampal volumes and increased whole brain atrophy.

An increased risk of dementia has been reported in relation to greater job demands (high job strain). 

Physical activity:  Benefits of physical exercise for vascular and cardio-metabolic health are well established. Increasing leisure-time physical activity goes hand in hand with social and cognitive activities, this could promote neuroprotection, providing additional protection against dementia. 

Lifestyle:   Unhealthy diet, alcohol misuse, smoking, obesity, hypertension, diabetes can lead to cardiovascular and cerebrovascular disorders, which can lead to depression or social isolation, which can lead to dementia.

  • Late life [> 60 years of age]

Social network:   Larger supportive social networks are confirmed to have beneficial effects. This involves structural and functional aspects (e.g. marital status, number of close contacts) and also subjective appraisals of social situation (e.g. feelings of loneliness). There is an improved protection involving care and support, reduced stress, economic resources, promotion of healthy lifestyles and behaviors and mental stimulation, all decreasing risk of dementia. 

Leisure activities (mental, physical and social):   Physical leisure time in late-life promotes the maintenance of brain function, even in presence of brain damage. Participation frequency and intensity of engagement of mentally, socially and physically stimulation activities play a role in maintaining better cognition.


Lifelong accumulated factors can in one way help to keep your cognitive function or in another way increase risk of dementia in aging. Negative life events during childhood, poor education, high job strain and work pressure, feelings of loneliness may increase risk of dementia, while in comparison a higher education level, good balance between work load and authority (job control), close contacts, socially, mentally and physical leisure activities may decrease risk of dementia.
