#SleepyTeens

The impact of social media on sleep quality and other conditions

March 19, 2021 Rebecca Tailleu PODCAST
  • #SleepyTeens Q&A
  • Rebecca Taillieu
  • 5:34
  • #SleepyTeens Q&A
  • Rebecca Taillieu
  • / 5:34

97% of the Scottish participants indicated that they use social media. Clearly, ussing social media sites (such as Facebook, Twitter, Instagram) is a central part of young people’s lives, day and night! 

This study examined how
1. Overall social media
2. Nighttime-social media
3. Emotional investment* (SMUIS)
is related to
1) Sleep quality (PSQI)
2) Anxiety & Depression (HADS)
3) Self-esteem (RSES)4

Three main statements were found in this study: 

1) 35% were classed as poor sleepers on the PSQI. Poor sleep was most strongly associated with nighttime-social media.

So timing of social media use is an important factor – specifically bedtime and during the night. Increased internet use is associated with shorter sleep duration, later bedtimes and rise times, longer sleep latencies and increased daytime tiredness in adolescents. Incoming alerts of text messages during the night disturbs the sleep of teens, but they also create a considerable pressure to be available related to fear of missing out. Young adults experience anxiety when texting is restricted and feel stressed and guilty when they do not reply immediately to a message. Therefore it is possible that young people struggle to relax at bedtime and results in poor sleep quality. 

2) Participants who scored positive on the HADS and RSES showed that this was strongly associated with emotional investment in social media.

Interpersonal feedback an social comparisons via social media will have strong effect on self-esteem levels who feel stronger emotionally connected to social media sites. Receiving positive or negative feedback on an online profile can increase or decrease self-esteem, while viewing one’s own profile can increase self-esteem. More frequent Facebook use has been linked to lower self-esteem in adults, due to increased exposure to social comparisons. Furthermore, adolescence is a vulnerable period where individuals are at risk for low self-esteem. Increased exposure to other users’ carefully constructed profiles that emphasize their positive characteristics diminish adolescents own feelings of self-worth. Findings that the timing of adolescents social media use and the emotional connection they have to sites is more important factor then simply the frequency or duration of social media use.

3) Lower self-esteem and increased levels of anxiety and depression were associated with poor sleep quality.

Poor sleep quality is prevalent in adolescents and can contribute to depression, anxiety and low self-esteem. Adolescence is a period of increased vulnerability for low self-esteem and onset of depression and anxiety. Teens who spend more time online and use social media sites tend to experience higher levels of anxiety and depression. Night-time specific social media use, poor sleep quality and anxiety and depression are in line with the idea that adolescents’ late night social media use results in later bedtimes and poorer sleep, which contributes to anxiety and depression. Anxiety is known to interfere with sleep, anxious adolescents may use social media more at night when they are unable to sleep. Those experiencing poor mental health may turn to social media in bed as a sleep aid or to regulate mood.
