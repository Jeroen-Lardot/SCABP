we need to preserve Earth’s remaining biodiversity

Why biodiversity is so important for us.

March 19, 2021 Rebecca Taillieu ARTICLE

Humans are continually altering Earth’s ecosystems. Developed and rapidly developing countries present changes like land to agriculture, more urban area, increased energy use, climate change and biotic homogenization. This creates new challenges for biodiversity conservation. We need interventions to decrease pathogens to spill over from wildlife to humans for our own well-being. 

This article firstly makes the reader more aware of the urgent need to act. We are in need of an increase our knowledge on the disturbance of habitat, which impacts species diversity and creates transmission of infections. For example, increasing forest fragmentation decreases host diversity and may cause increase disease risk for lime disease (Borrelia burgdorferi) or WNE (West Nile encephalitis). 

Secondly the article summarized research of biodiversity conservation interventions into public health tools. Some methods are relatively cheap, including hand washing, using insecticide-treated bed-nets for mosquito-borne diseases or checking oneself for ticks. Others require action that come with substantial costs, including vaccination, drug treatment and vector control. Furthermore, some biodiversity conservation interventions may benefit long-term public health: setting aside land for protected areas and protect natural areas, altering urban planning, large-scale restoration to reduce forest fragmentation, species reintroductions. 

The article ends with a cry out to develop greater interventions to detect interactions between biodiversity-disease processes. More studies are needed. Although the conclusion is clear: biodiversity conservation interventions may reduce the total burden of infectious diseases. In addition, they have impacts on human health and well-being. Well-being includes physical, mental and social aspects and is not merely the absence of disease. Increasing Earth’s biodiversity may have positive effects on several aspects of well-being, including both physical and mental health benefits associated with spending time in nature. If we want to walk ‘safe’ in nature, we need to understand more about taking care of the remaining Earth’s biodiversity.
