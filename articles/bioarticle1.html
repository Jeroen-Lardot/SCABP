Even monkeys want to be treated equal

A study of equality in monkeys

March 19, 2021 Jieh-Jang Chen PODCAST
  • Equal Pay in Monkeys Q&A
  • Jieh-Jang Chen
  • 3:44
  • Equal Pay in Monkeys Q&A
  • Jieh-Jang Chen
  • / 3:44

Cooperation is one of the of the special traits human use to build such a highly advance society. It has been speculated that during evolution of cooperation, individual would compare their own efforts and pay-offs with those of others and a negative reaction would be shown if one’s expectation of pay-offs is not met. This can explain how humans will only cooperative if one feels that one is treated reasonably. However, we are not the only animals that are know to cooperative, in fact many examples such as a herd of zebras protecting themselves from lions. Does inequality aversion also lie in other animals? It seems highly possible. In this research, Frans de Waal demonstrates with brown capuchin monkeys how they can also perceive fairness.

Female brown capuchin monkeys were tested in four different condition, ET, equality test; IT, inequality test; EC, effort control; FC, food control. For all the test, a monkey was required to give a token to the experimenter and in exchange will receive a cucumber. For ET, a partner monkey is nearby and preforms the same action but receives a cucumber. For IT, the partner monkey will receive a grape instead of a cucumber, and for EC a partner will not need to complete any action but still receive a grape. Lastly, in FC, no partner was present but a grape was put in place of the partner monkey. For equality test, nearly all exchange of tokens were completed, however for all the other test, a large percentage of the exchanges failed with the EC being the most failed.

In this experiment, brown capuchin monkeys view grapes as a better reward than cucumbers. During ET with no other monkey and reward to compare to, the monkey is perfectly happy with the exchange. However, when other monkey doing the same exchange get a better prize, or without even any other action get a better prize, than the monkey would throw away the cucumber given in a protest of inequality. This shows that the monkey understands that the basic concept of fairness, and also, it’s desire to be treated fairly. This may also mean that monkeys like humans respond by social emotions. These emotions, known as ‘passions’ by economists, guide human reactions to the efforts, gains, losses and attitudes of others. These reactions have

thought to have evolved to promote long term human cooperation. Learning more about how other animals perceive morality, might help us humans learn on the behaviour of our own species.

Next time you feel like you are treated unfairly, remember that even a monkey stands up to for its’ own equality, so should you.
